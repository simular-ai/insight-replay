Stateful Reasoning via Insight Replay

Bin Lei1, Caiwen Ding1, Jiachen Yang2, Ang Li2, Xin Eric Wang2
1University of Minnesota, 2Simular AI
Contact: leib2765@gmail.com  ·  eric@simular.ai
Paper arXiv Code

InsightReplay periodically extracts critical insights from the reasoning trace and replays them near the active generation frontier, shifting the accuracy peak to longer chains and raising peak performance.

Motivation

Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting has become a foundation for eliciting reasoning in large language models, and the recent wave of test-time scaling takes this further: pushing the reasoning-token budget higher often translates into higher accuracy on hard problems.

But several recent studies converge on a less optimistic picture: longer CoT is not monotonically better. On a fixed-difficulty problem, accuracy typically traces an inverted-U as CoT length grows — first improving, peaking, then declining once the chain becomes excessively long (Wu et al. 2025; Chen et al. 2026; Gema et al. 2025). Harder problems shift this peak rightward, but the decline itself is robust.

That raises a sharp question: what drives the decline? Why does the model get worse at a problem the more it thinks about it? Prior work has identified that not all reasoning tokens contribute equally — a small subset of sentences disproportionately determines the final answer, and certain attention heads consistently focus on them (Bogdan et al. 2025). We pick up from there with a different question: how does the model's access to such critical insights evolve as the reasoning chain grows?

Preliminary Experiments

We probe long-CoT traces with two controlled experiments and surface two properties of critical insights — the small set of compressed sub-conclusions scattered through a reasoning trace.

Finding 1 — Insights and the reasoning trace are complementary

On 60 AIME problems with Qwen3-8B, we generate full thinking traces (max 30K tokens), then extract 5–7 key insights from each. We feed the model seven contents inside the <think> tag and compute the answer-probability P(ans) in a single forward pass:

Two things stand out. (i) Insights carry concentrated signal. Condition C retains 75.6% of the answer probability with only 1.4% of the tokens. (ii) Insights are additive on top of the trace. Conditions D / E (trace + insights replayed) outperform A (trace alone): even when the reasoning is already in context, re-exposing the model to its own insights yields measurable gains.

Finding 2 — Attention to critical insights decays with distance

We measure how the pre-softmax attention score from the answer token to a critical insight varies as the insight is pushed further from the generation frontier. To isolate distance from content, we insert semantically neutral filler tokens (newline + space) between insight and answer at insertion ratio ρ ∈ {0, 0.1, 0.2, 0.3, 0.4}, giving total distance (1+ρ)d. From ρ = 0 to ρ = 0.4, attention to critical insights drops 19.2% on Qwen3-8B (paired bootstrap, p < 0.001) and 3.3% on Gemma-4-31B-it. The decay is monotonic and survives a very different RoPE configuration (Gemma-4 uses partial-rotary RoPE on only the first quarter of head dimensions), suggesting this is a general property of trained attention rather than a feature of any specific positional encoding.

Math Modeling

Following Wu et al. (2025), for a task of difficulty $T$ and a model with reasoning capability $M$, the probability that an $N$-step CoT produces the correct final answer is

$A_0(N) \;=\; \alpha \, \bigl[\,(1-\sigma(T))\,(1 - T/(NM))\,\bigr]^{\,N}\!,$

where $\sigma(T)$ is the probability of mis-generating the next sub-question and $T/(NM)$ is the per-step error rate. Crucially, this parameterization drops the dependence on history: each step is assumed accurate independently of its position in the chain.

Our Finding 2 says otherwise: as step index $i$ grows, the model's ability to attend to earlier critical insights diminishes. We restore this missing dependence by introducing an insight accessibility function $\Phi(i)$ into the sub-question term, modeling sub-question accuracy at step $i$ as $(1-\sigma(T))\cdot\Phi(i)$, with

$\Phi(0)=1,\quad \Phi\;\text{strictly decreasing},\quad \lim_{i\to\infty}\Phi(i)=0.$

This gives two accuracy curves — the real world (with decay) and InsightReplay, which relocates critical insights to a fixed distance $d_0$ at every step:

$A_\Phi(N) = [p(N)]^N \cdot \prod_{i=1}^{N}\Phi(i), \qquad A_{\mathrm{IR}}(N) = \bigl[\,p(N)\cdot\Phi(d_0)\,\bigr]^N,$

where $p(N) := (1-\sigma(T))(1-T/(NM))$ is the per-step accuracy without decay. Because $\Phi(d_0)$ is a positive constant in $A_{\mathrm{IR}}$ while $\Phi(i)$ inside $A_\Phi$ is driven to zero, two consequences follow under mild conditions: (i) InsightReplay shifts the optimal CoT length $N^*$ rightward — the model can productively think for longer before degradation kicks in, and (ii) InsightReplay raises the achievable peak accuracy. The full proof is in the paper.

Method

InsightReplay treats reasoning as a stateful process. The reasoning state at any point is the cumulative set of insights the model has generated so far — compressed abstractions of prior reasoning. Periodically, the model extracts new insights and replays them near the active generation frontier, so that critical intermediate content remains accessible despite the natural attention decay that accompanies long reasoning chains.

Results

Across all 24 settings of the {model-scale} × {model-family} × {benchmark} grid, 3-round InsightReplay improves accuracy over the standard chain-of-thought baseline. Averaged improvement is +1.65 points, with the largest single-setting gain reaching +9.2 points on R1-Distill-32B's LiveCodeBench v5 subset.
